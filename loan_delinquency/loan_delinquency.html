Loan Delinquency

🔹 Step 1: Introduction and Business Context

🎯 Objective of the Project

This project simulates a realistic loan portfolio and borrower behavior to study loan delinquency. Using synthetic data generated with statistical logic and the Faker library, we model how different borrower profiles influence the likelihood of missed payments. The simulation serves as a foundation for building risk models and analyzing credit behavior in the banking domain.

🏦 Business Context: Why Loan Delinquency Matters

In banking and lending institutions, understanding borrower behavior is critical to managing credit risk. One key indicator of borrower risk is loan delinquency — when a borrower misses scheduled payments.

A delinquent loan refers to a loan where the borrower has missed one or more payments, but the loan hasn’t yet defaulted.

Delinquency is an early warning signal of potential default.

Banks and lenders use delinquency metrics to:

  • Adjust credit policies
  • Refine credit scoring models
  • Launch early intervention strategies (e.g., calls, repayment plans)
  • Manage capital provisioning (reserving money for potential losses)
  • Optimize collections and recovery processes

🧠 What is Delinquency?

A loan becomes delinquent when the borrower fails to pay on the scheduled date.

Delinquency is typically measured in buckets:

  • 0–30 days: Mild delinquency
  • 31–60 days: Moderate risk
  • 61–90+ days: Severe risk or default likely

In this simulation, we define delinquency as any loan with at least one missed payment over the observed period.

📉 What Causes Delinquency?

  • Low credit score or poor credit history
  • Over-borrowing relative to income
  • High interest rates increasing EMI burden
  • Job loss, emergencies, or economic downturns
  • Lack of financial discipline

🧩 How This Project Helps

This simulation provides a sandbox for analyzing delinquency behavior and supports:

  • Credit Risk Modeling: Use the simulated dataset to build Probability of Default (PD) models. Train logistic regression or XGBoost classifiers to predict delinquency.

  • Policy Testing: Simulate what happens if credit policies change (e.g., stricter loan limits for low-score borrowers).
  • Portfolio Monitoring: Use the generated data to test dashboards and delinquency trackers for operational use.

🔹 Data Generation Using Faker and Statistical Logic

🎯 Objective of This Step

To create a realistic dataset representing loan accounts and borrower behavior over time. The dataset simulates various features relevant to loan delinquency modeling, such as borrower income, credit score, loan amount, and payment patterns.

🛠️ Data Simulation Approach

We use the Faker library to generate synthetic data, ensuring that the values resemble real-world distributions. Beyond just generating random data, we apply statistical logic and domain knowledge to craft a dataset that mimics actual borrower behavior.

👤 Borrower Profile Features

Each loan account in the dataset includes the following borrower-level attributes:

  • Customer ID: Unique identifier for each borrower.
  • Income: Monthly income, simulated with a log-normal distribution.
  • Credit Score: Assigned using a custom logic to reflect realistic credit distributions (ranges from 300 to 850).
  • Loan Amount: Simulated based on income, ensuring proportional borrowing.
  • Loan Term: Typically 12, 24, or 36 months.
  • Interest Rate: Varies inversely with credit score to reflect risk-based pricing.
  • EMI (Equated Monthly Installment): Calculated based on loan amount, term, and interest.
  • Start Date: Loan initiation month.
  • Demographic Variables: Age, employment type, region, etc.

📆 Monthly Grid Creation

We simulate a month-by-month view for each borrower using an exploded grid:

  • For each month, we track:
    • Payment Status (Paid/Missed)
    • DPD (Days Past Due) Bucket
    • Outstanding Balance
  • Payment behavior is linked to:
    • Credit score
    • Income-to-EMI ratio
    • Random shocks (e.g., job loss simulation)

⚙️ Missed Payment Simulation Logic

We define rules to simulate delinquency:

  • Borrowers with low credit scores or tight income-to-EMI ratios have higher chances of missing payments.
  • Payment status is assigned using probabilistic thresholds:
    • High score + high income → ~95% chance of on-time payment
    • Low score + high EMI → ~40–70% chance of delay or miss
  • Missed payments accumulate into DPD buckets.

📊 Delinquency Flag Definition

A borrower is considered delinquent if any monthly record shows a missed payment or DPD > 0. This label is used for training and evaluating models later.

🔍 Example Simulation Rule

If a borrower has:

  • Income: ₹25,000
  • EMI: ₹15,000
  • Credit Score: 520

Then the chance of missing payment = 60% each month.

If 3 consecutive months are missed → borrower enters 60+ DPD bucket.

Instead of a flat dataset, this time series simulation adds realism:

  • Enables transition tracking (from current → 30 DPD → 60 DPD → default)
  • Allows testing of credit policies and scoring models under realistic conditions
  • Sets the stage for predictive modeling in upcoming steps

🔹 Tagging Borrower Delinquency Statuses

🎯 Objective of This Step

In this step, we assign a delinquency status to each borrower for every month based on their payment history. These statuses simulate real-world borrower risk progression and are essential for modeling and analysis.

📌 Why Tagging Matters

Instead of just labeling whether a borrower paid or not, we simulate how borrowers move through different stages of delinquency over time. This provides richer behavior patterns for modeling credit risk and borrower lifecycle events.

📊 Status Definitions

  • Current – The borrower has made all payments up to that month (0 missed payments).
  • 30 DPD – The borrower has missed one monthly payment (30 days past due).
  • 60 DPD – The borrower has missed two consecutive payments.
  • 90+ DPD / Default – The borrower has missed three or more consecutive payments. This is considered as defaulted.

Once a borrower enters Default, they remain in that state permanently for the simulation, regardless of future payments.

⚙️ Tagging Logic

To determine the delinquency status, we calculate a Days Past Due (DPD) metric for every borrower-month using the following logic:

  • DPD = 0 → Status = Current
  • DPD ≥ 30 and < 60 → Status = 30 DPD
  • DPD ≥ 60 and < 90 → Status = 60 DPD
  • DPD ≥ 90 → Status = Default

Once a borrower reaches Default, all following months remain Default regardless of future payments. This mimics how many institutions treat defaulted loans — no reversion unless restructured or written off (not modeled here).

🔄 Transition Behavior

Borrowers transition through risk states as they miss consecutive payments. For example:

Current → 30 DPD → 60 DPD → Default

This logic reflects credit risk escalation in real portfolios. We maintain the history of past payment behavior so the model can learn how risk accumulates.

🧪 Example

Jan: Paid → Status: Current

Feb: Missed → Status: 30 DPD

Mar: Missed again → Status: 60 DPD

Apr: Missed again → Status: Default

May: Paid → Still remains in Default

Even though the borrower paid in May, the status doesn't reset, highlighting that risk accumulation is sticky in delinquency modeling.

📦 Final Output Structure

After tagging, our dataset now includes for every borrower-month:

  • Borrower ID
  • Calendar Month
  • Actual Payment Indicator
  • Calculated DPD
  • Final Delinquency Status Label

This tagged status is the foundation for our downstream modeling (e.g., predicting who will default next month).

🔍 Modeling Borrower Default Risk

🎯 Objective of This Step

Now that we have monthly borrower behavior data and delinquency statuses, the goal is to train a model to predict which borrowers are likely to default in the upcoming months. This is the core of credit risk modeling and simulates how lenders make proactive decisions.

📌 Target Variable

The target variable for our model is:

Default in Next Month (Yes/No)
This binary variable is created by shifting the “Default” status one month forward for each borrower. If a borrower defaults in March, their label in February will be 1 (positive), indicating default is coming. All other months are labeled 0.

This setup ensures the model learns to recognize early signals of rising risk before actual default happens.

🧠 Predictive Features

We use features from the current and past behavior of each borrower to train our model. Example features include:

  • Number of missed payments in the past 3 months
  • Previous month's delinquency status
  • Count of months since first delinquency
  • Whether the borrower has ever been 30 or 60 DPD before
  • Rolling average of recent payment activity
These features capture both short-term and long-term risk patterns.

🔄 Feature Engineering Logic

We perform transformations to create predictive features:

  • Rolling windows (e.g., number of missed payments in last 2–3 months)
  • Flag variables (e.g., ever reached 60 DPD)
  • Cumulative default indicators
  • One-hot encoding of current status (Current, 30 DPD, 60 DPD)
Each transformation adds temporal depth and mimics how banks track customer deterioration over time.

⚙️ Model Setup

We use a logistic regression model for interpretability and simplicity, though this setup can easily be extended to decision trees or ensemble models later.

  • Target: Will borrower default next month? (1 or 0)
  • Inputs: Engineered features described above
  • Training Data: Borrower-month records before the month of prediction
  • Evaluation: Standard train/test split, accuracy, precision, recall, and ROC AUC

✅ Interpreting Model Output

Logistic regression will return a probability score (between 0 and 1) for each borrower-month.

We set a classification threshold (e.g., 0.5 or adjusted via ROC curve) to assign:

  • Default risk = High → If predicted probability ≥ threshold
  • Default risk = Low → Otherwise
This output can help simulate risk segmentation:
  • Flag borrowers for early intervention
  • Compare model predictions vs actual defaults
  • Calibrate policies like grace periods or risk-based pricing

🧪 Example Scenario

If a borrower has:

  • Missed payments in Jan and Feb
  • Is currently in 60 DPD
  • Was Current 3 months ago
Then the model may assign a 0.82 probability of default in March.

This allows early flagging before default occurs, mimicking real-world risk strategies.

🧾 Final Modeling Output

The final dataset includes:

  • All original borrower-month data
  • Engineered features
  • Predicted default probability
  • Final model prediction (1 = predicted to default next month)
This output can be used for strategy simulation, dashboarding, or model evaluation.
